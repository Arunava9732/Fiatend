RISK WARNING DISCLOSURE

Virtual/crypto assets and their dealing come with a number of risks. You hereby indicate and guarantee that you have reviewed the following Virtual/Crypto Assets Risk Disclosures before accessing or using the Services.

Overview of virtual/crypto commodities –

Virtual/crypto assets have no intrinsic worth and are not recognised as legal tender in the majority of countries, including India. The agreement of the parties to a transaction, which may or may not be dependent on the market worth of the virtual/crypto asset at the moment of the transaction, determines the price of virtual/crypto assets. Since most nations do not have regulations governing investments in virtual or crypto-assets, you are not likely to be safeguarded if something goes awry. Additionally, the documentation released by the company that created the virtual/crypto-asset may not be entirely explicit about the risks involved with the purchase.

Market Rate Variations –

Virtual and crypto assets are extremely volatile because their prices are determined by how people consider them to be worth, as well as other elements that are beyond the power of any one entity.

Liquidity, valuation, and market influence-

Virtual/crypto-assets can be exchanged individually negotiated deals, through a wide range of virtual/crypto-asset exchanges, and through a number of middlemen worldwide, each with its own pricing model and/or order book. There are many valuation difficulties caused by the absence of a centralised pricing source. It may also affect values or availability that a small number of virtual/crypto assets are held in a highly concentrated manner. Thus, when purchasing or selling virtual or crypto-assets, you might not receive a reasonable price or treatment, or you might not be able to sell your virtual or crypto-assets as rapidly as you would like if there is no buyer. Furthermore, market players attempting to exit a stake may encounter difficulties due to the dispersed liquidity, especially during stressful times. There have been numerous reports of market fraud cases.

Cybersecurity-

Cybersecurity risks associated with virtual/crypto assets and related "wallets" or spot trades include hacking flaws and the possibility that openly disseminated ledgers may not be immutable. For market players who exchange virtual/crypto assets, a cyber-security incident could cause a sizable, instant, and irreversible loss. A virtual/crypto asset's price is likely to decline, possibly affecting other virtual/crypto commodities as well, if there is even a small cybersecurity incident.

Unidentified Spot Market Analysis-

Virtual/crypto asset amounts are usually stored as a block chain address that can be viewed using private keys, which may be kept by a custodian or a market player. On a blockchain or distributed ledger, virtual/cryptoasset activities are usually openly accessible, but the public address does not reveal the controller, proprietor, or user of the secret key. Virtual/crypto-asset exchanges and administrators that store virtual/crypto-assets do not always know who the proprietor is, in contrast to bank and brokerage accounts. The opaque underlying or spot market makes asset verification difficult for market participants, regulators, and auditors and increases the risk of manipulation and fraud, including the possibility of Ponzi schemes, bucket shops, and pump and dump schemes, which may erode market confidence in a virtual/crypto asset and have a negative impact on its price.

Intermediaries, administrators, and virtual/crypto asset exchanges-

Virtual/crypto asset exchanges are comparatively new and generally unregulated in most countries, as are other middlemen, custodians, and vendors used to enable virtual/crypto asset trades. The risk that a virtual/crypto-asset exchange may not hold enough virtual/crypto-assets and funds to fulfill its responsibilities and that such deficit may not be readily recognised or discovered is brought on by the hidden underlying spot market and the absence of regulatory supervision. Additionally, compared to controlled futures or stock exchanges, many virtual/crypto-asset exchanges have encountered major disruptions, downtime, transaction processing delays, flash breakdowns, and may have a greater degree of operational risk. Particularly in a cross-border setting where it may also be challenging to determine which laws may be applied, it may be difficult or even impossible to identify and/or find the issuer of virtual/crypto-asset, the trading platform, the wallet provider, or the intermediary. So, if a user has a claim, it might be challenging to bring legal action against the issuer or wallet service and establish ownership.

Administrative Setting-

Most virtual/crypto assets, as well as the marketing of goods or services associated with virtual/crypto assets, are unsupervised. In these circumstances, you will not be entitled to the rights and safeguards, such as protest or recourse procedures, that are accessible to customers of controlled financial services. The regulatory environment for virtual and cryptocurrency assets is presently ambiguous in many countries. Additionally, some foreign state securities authorities have issued warnings that many initial coin offerings are probably covered by their respective securities laws and will therefore likely come under the definition of a security. In the future, one or more states may pass laws, rules, or guidelines that have an impact on virtual/crypto asset networks and their users. The price of virtual/crypto assets and their adoption by users, retailers, and service providers may be impacted by such laws, rules, or orders.

Technology-

The technology that underpins virtual/crypto-assets is relatively new and is changing quickly, which adds specific risks. For instance, in order to view, use, or trade a virtual/cryptoasset on a block chain or distributed ledger, a distinct private key is necessary. A virtual/crypto object linked to a private key may experience an irrevocable loss if it is lost, stolen, or destroyed. Investors' capacity to engage in forks may have consequences as well. For instance, if a virtual/cryptoasset exchange forbids its clients from taking part in a fork that produces a new product, a market participant having a stake through the exchange may suffer.

Transaction Fees-

Many virtual/crypto assets enable traders to pay processors (i.e., parties that handle transactions and log them on a distributed database or blockchain) a fee. Although it is not required, a fee is typically required to guarantee that a transaction is quickly documented on a blockchain or distributed database. These costs' amounts are determined by market factors, and it is possible that they will rise significantly in a time of worry. Additionally, compared to caretakers in many other financial marketplaces, virtual/crypto asset exchanges, wallet providers, and other custodians may charge excessive costs.

Inadequate Details-

Any particular virtual or cryptocurrency asset's information could be absent, incorrect, lacking, or confusing in terms of the project and its risks. To comprehend the features of the virtual/crypto-asset and/or the project, you may need to have advanced knowledge and a thorough understanding of the documents. Some virtual/crypto assets and associated goods are aggressively marketed to the general public using marketing collateral and other information that may be ambiguous, lacking crucial details, unreliable, or even deliberately deceptive.

Fraud & Malicious Activities-

There are a lot of scams and false virtual/crypto assets out there, and you should be conscious that their only goal is to steal your money using various methods, like phishing or using a bogus ID. A significant amount of financial risk is involved when trading in digital payment tokens. Digital Payment Tokens might not be suitable for all buyers, so you should, if required, seek out fair guidance. Trading with digital payment tokens carries the risk of losing all of your money.
